PRESS RELEASE
 
OMCT hosts three-day training for Libyan prison
officials
For
Immediate Release
4
May 2014
 
TRIPOLI – Prisons across Libya remain largely
unregulated and vulnerable to corruption, and prison management and personnel
are often eager for professional training but do not know where to turn. To
bridge this gap, OMCT begins a pilot training course today entitled, Raising Awareness of Prison Officials on
Human Rights Standards and Building the Capacity of State Institutions to
Guarantee the Humane Treatment of Detainees in Libya. This three-day workshop
features fifty participants who serve with
the Judicial Police (Ministry of Justice), the Department for Combating Illegal
Migration (Ministry of Interior), and the Military Police (Ministry of Defense)
in Tripoli, Benghazi, Sabha, Zawiya, Zintan, and five other
cities, as well as expert trainers from the United Nations Support Mission in
Libya (UNSMIL). 
 
OMCT’s goal for this workshop is to build
ground-level buy-in to the idea that torture is never acceptable. Currun Singh,
Regional Advisor for OMCT, said, “This training is as much about the rule of
law as it is about basic common sense. The question here is, how do we get
detention centers under state control operating to minimal standards and how
can we help the people running these detention centers to make that happen?”
 
The featured speakers from UNSMIL, Mr.
Khaled Al Majali and Mr.
Urwah Alhabahbeh, are international corrections experts who train
personnel on prison management and UN minimum rules for the treatment of
detainees. OMCT, in partnership with Mr. Al Majali and Mr. Alhabahbeh, has
developed a comprehensive curriculum for the workshop, which will engage
participants on the following topics over three days: the prohibition of
torture and ill-treatment as per Libyan, regional, and international law; the rights
of detainees and the importance of due process; proper procedures for arrest
and investigation; the obligations attendant to the use of force and firearms; and
the ethical responsibility to refuse illegal orders. Each topic will be presented
and broken down, whereupon participants will separate into small groups to debate
mock scenarios and formulate solutions for review and critique by the UNSMIL
experts. Mr. Al Majali and Mr. Alhabahbeh said, “We are pleased to participate
with OMCT in this training course to raise the awareness and qualifications of
Libyan prison officials. This course will have a great impact on changing the
behavior of staff to become more professional and skillful at their job and to
improve the overall competence of the penitentiary system.”
 
OMCT has been on the ground in Libya
since February 2012 as part of a national anti-torture project that documents violations
and increases access to justice for vulnerable populations. Since April 2013,
when the General National Congress adopted a law criminalizing torture,
discrimination, and enforced disappearance, OMCT has assembled a nationwide
network of lawyers to document and treat cases and mobilized civil society
organizations to monitor and implement the law. There is also a certain
willingness on the part of public institutions to reassert their authority and
create secure routes to justice within their jurisdictions. But despite these
advancements, practices of torture and ill-treatment have democratized across
the country in the three years since the revolution. There remain countless
numbers of men, women, and children who linger in detention under torture or
threat of torture, and perpetrators enjoy impunity all the while. It is with
this in mind that OMCT is redoubling its energies to prevent torture at the
source, by assisting the Libyan state to formalize and professionalize its
security sector. 
 
During the course, OMCT will also
welcome observers from civil society organizations, foreign embassies,
international NGOs, and the media. We look forward to an illuminating and
constructive three days with all of our invited guests, and we welcome your
feedback and questions upon the completion of this workshop.

لجنة حقوق الإنسان بالأمم المتحدة و من بعد الثورة بدأت العمل من داخل ليبيا منذ

القانون. هناك أيضا رغبة معينة من جانب المؤسسات العامة لتأكيد سلطتهم و إنشاء مسارات
